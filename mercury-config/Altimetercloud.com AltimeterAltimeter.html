▼ Pressure & altitude
Forecast pressure
The forecast pressure is the local weather forecast pressure at sea level. Entering this for your launch site will make your altitude readings a little bit more accurate. It's not absolutly essential as you will still get decent accuracy without.

In the UK you can find these pressures on a website called xcweather.co.uk, although you will need to put your phone on the side to see the column you require if it's not visible.

Oversampling
Oversampling is the method by which the pressure sensor can increase it's accuracy. It does it by performing multiple samples before outputting the result, the BMP390 sensor also adds one bit of accuracty for each step up in the oversampling option. We suggest keeping this on the 4X Oversampling option for best results.
IIR Filter
The IIR filter is the BMP390's internal Infinite Impulse Response filter. It is used to dampen sudden pressure changes caused by gusts of wind or ejection charges by comparing both old values to current values.

Low values will result in more noise in the output, and higher values will have less noise but add a very minute lag to extreme sudden changes. We suggest keeping this on settings 1, 3 or 7.
Pressure filter
The final pressure filter is fed with the result from the Oversampling and IIR filter options on the sensor. We offer the use of a Kalman filter on the pressure samples. Option 1 offers the lease filtering and fastes response, option 2 is the standard option and option 3 offers a higher amount of filtering. You can also turn off the Kalman filter if you are looking to output raw data to process yourself later.
Temp sensor
If you have installed one of our external MT1 temperature sensors you can automatically use it's temperature at launch for calculating the altitude by enabling this setting. You can use this to log the temperature or also use the temperature at launch for altitude calculations.
Static temperature
The static calculation temperature has a default value of 15 degrees celcius. You can have the altitude calculated on a different ambient temperature if you choose by setting it here.

▼ Launch & detection
Launch detect
The launch detect setting is how high an altitude the altimeter must see to confirm that the rocket has launched. We suggest sticking to 10m for most uses here to avoid a strong gust of wind accidently triggering the rocket (although this is unlikely even at 5m). The 2m setting is handy for testing your altimeter indoors or by throwing it in the air, but shouldn't be used for general rocket launches.
Launch ALP
Accidental launch protection helps to prevent a gust of wind or even you pulling your nose cone off triggering the altimeters pressure based altitude sensor. Anything that causes a sudden pressure drop looks like an altitude rise to the altimeter. Accidental launch protection requires the acceleration magnitude to exceed the set target for 3 of the previous 8 samples when the altitude exceeds the launch detect settting rather than soley relying on the pressure. If your rocket is just sat on the pad waiting for launch this will prevent it triggering accidently.
Launch lock
Startup lock prevents the altimeter from detecting a launch until this number of seconds has passed since you turn the device on. It's not needed typically and for most uses it's best to leave this set to 0.

Lockout time
Milliseconds to lock out apogee and landing detection and flatline altitude after a lockout trigger. Set to 0 to disable. Default: 500ms.

Lockout change
Altitude drop in metres per sample at 100 Hz that triggers a lockout. Automatically doubled at 50 Hz to maintain the same m/s sensitivity. Catches ejection charge events and supersonic transitions. Default: 1.00m.

▼ Recording
Sensor speed
The base sensor cycle rate. 100Hz gives double the data resolution during flight but uses slightly more battery. 50Hz is the power-saving option.
Sample ratio
The altimeter runs at 50Hz however you can record every sample or every X samples in order to extend the log time. For example if you set the ratio to 1 it will save 1:1 samples, or if you set it to 3 it will save 1 in 3 samples.
Max samples
This setting will stop the recording when this number of samples has been reached. You may find this useful if you disable the auto stop function and want to limit the time of a recording.
Recording stop
The recording stop option lets you decide how and when to stop your flight recording data. You can select either 300 or 600 samples stable (altitude doesn't change more than 1 meter for 99% of these samples), or you can choose to record until stopped manually by pressing the BUTTON.

The device will still stop itself when it reaches the maximum 10200 sample limit regardless of which setting you choose.
▼ IMU & orientation
Sync sensors
The Sync sensors option works to put the accelerometer, gyroscope and altimeter in sync with each other. The accelerometer/gryo data can end up being reported sooner than the altimeter due to the filters on the pressure sensor by around 100-300ms. On launch detection the Sync sensors option will look back to when the accelerometer first saw the launch and check the difference in time between the sensors. It will then adjust the accelerometer data to match the altimeter data.
IMU Filter
The fusion filter is how the altimeter works out it's orientation angles from the Accelerometer and Gyroscope readings. As the Mercury does not have a magnetometer we have to use 6DOF and some errors will build up over time while in flight.These filters along with the Gyroscope calibration help minimise these errors and give great results.
Orientation
The orientation of your sensor in your rocket needs to be specified if you want to use angles such as tilt, roll, pitch and yaw. Or to view them in your data logs and have them be accurate. The standard device upright (USB port down) is the first setting, followed by the device pointing downards with the USB port upwards. The 3rd option is the device on it's side with the I2C port pointing upwards. The 4th option is the device on it's side with the I2C port pointing downwards. Finally the 5th setting is the device on it's back facing upwards, and the 6th setting is the device on it's front facing downwards.

▼ Power
WiFi shutdown
When in WiFi mode your device will consume more power and will become warm, this is completly normal, however to avoid leaving the device on forever it will be turned off in WiFi mode after the set amount of time. Just turn it back on when you need, it only takes a few seconds to boot up. This does not effect flight mode, only WiFi mode.
WiFi TX power
Higher TX power improves range but uses more battery. Default 5dBm is a good balance. 17dBm is maximum range.
UART output
Enable serial data output on the GP pads for external devices. Leave disabled unless you have hardware connected.
After flight
What happens after a flight is saved. Shutdown powers off the device. eNOW mode sends periodic ESP-NOW pings for ground tracking.
eNOW interval
How often the device sends an ESP-NOW ping after flight when eNOW mode is active.
eNOW on startup
If enabled, eNOW pings start immediately on power up instead of only after a flight.
Smart power mode
Power saving mode while waiting on the pad. eMode 1 runs sensors at 50Hz. eMode 2 uses 25Hz CPU wake with FIFO batch-read for lower power, disables the battery bar and blinks the status LED every 8 seconds. Both snap to full speed on motion.
